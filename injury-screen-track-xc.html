Hamstring Mobility & Strength

The hamstring muscles run along the back of the leg from the pelvis to the knee. They play a major role in running, jumping and throwing because they allow you to extend your hips and bend your knees. The hamstrings can become shortened from long periods of sitting and repetitive movements like running. Lack of hamstring flexibility is often associated with uneven pelvic alignment and all types of issues down the chain, including knee pain. If your hamstrings aren't strong enough to handle the exercise volume or intensity you're asking of them, you are at risk for straining or pulling them.

Spine Awareness & Core Strength

The "core" includes the abdominals, back and glutes. These muscles play a major role in maintaining proper pelvic alignment, posture and biomechanics. People who spend a lot of time sitting often have weak or inactive glutes. If the glutes are not able to do their job during exercise, they will push the work to other muscles, which can overload them and cause injuries. Having a strong core and good spinal awareness also helps the knees track correctly during movement, reducing the risk of common running injuries like IT Band Syndrome and runner's knee.

Ankle Mobility & Stability

The way your ankles move when your feet contact the ground dictates how forces are transferred up your legs when you run, jump or throw. To reduce the risk of pain and injury, you want your ankles to have the right balance of mobility and stability. Running on feet or ankles that are either too rigid or unstable increases the likelihood that you will suffer nagging injuries like tendonitis, plantar fasciitis or shin splints. Foot and ankle problems can cause compensation issues at the knees and/or hips as your body tries to absorb the force of each stride. Those with a history of ankle sprain should be especially mindful of their ankle stability (or lack thereof). If not properly rehabilitated, even a low-grade ankle sprain can result in serious injury months or years later.
